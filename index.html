Beyond Total Sleep Time: Fitbits for Circadian Rhythm Analysis

Developed python library to generate quantitative measures of sleep-activity rhythm stability and timing using Fitbit step count data (2020)

Data was obtained from adult participants who simultaneously wore a Fitbit and a lab-grade wrist accelerometer (Actiwatch 2.0) on opposite arms. Arm assignment was randomized across participants.

To characterize participant's rest-activity rhythms, we used both extended cosinor analysis, which involves modelling an individual’s typical 24-hour rest-activity rhythm as a square-like sinusoidal wave (Marler, 2006), and non-parametric analysis (Blume, 2016; Goncalves, 2014; Witting, 1990), which refrains from making any assumptions about the shape of an individual’s typical rhythm. These methods provide key metrics which are reflective of different aspects of the rest-activity rhythm, including the magnitude, phase, and stability.

Because the devices use different sensitivity thresholds for detecting motion, we normalized each subject's activity values by dividing by the maximum activity value across their recording period for each device before performing statistical analyses.

When activity counts are normalized, the Actiwatch and Fitbit data look pretty similar during active periods. When we look at the rest (REST) and sleep (REST-S) periods however, Fitbit is more sensitive to activity. This can be seen more clearly in the graph below, where we look at the difference in activity levels between the Actiwatch and Fitbit according to the interval type (Active, Rest, and Sleep). The differences in rest and sleep are largely negative, meaning the Fitbit activity is estimated to be greater than Actiwatch activity during those periods.

How can the patterns in the data be summarized with quantitative metrics? We applied an anti-logistic transformed sinusoidal model to the log of the maximum activity level at each minute across seven days of activity recording. Because the model is transformed with an anti-logistic function, this gives the model a more square-like shape, which more closely mimics 24-hour human activity patterns.

Model fit is shown below for activity count data obtained from the Actiwatch and the Fitbit for the same participant.

From these models, we can extract parameters that reflect different aspects of daily patterns of sleep and activity. For example, the minimum of the curve indicates restlessness during sleep, where a higher minimum means more activity during the sleep period. Another key metric is the acrophase, or the time of day corresponding to the peak of the curve. This is used as a measure of phase, and can be a good indicator of whether a person is a morning or evening person (chronotype). The amplitude of the rhythm is another important metric, because it indexes how stable the rhythm is from day to day. Larger amplitudes arise from more consistent timing of wake, sleep, and peak activity, whereas reduced amplitudes result from lack of activity consistency.

These measures reflect the earlier observation that the Actiwatch has a higher minimum (actmin) relative to the Fitbit, which may be driven by the Actiwatch's higher sensitivity during the Active period. The other parameters appear to be relatively consistent. Group-level analyses will be important for determining whether there are meaningful differences in these measures across devices.

K-Means in the Wild West

Optimizing K-means clustering parameters for image segmentation on West Texas photographs (2021)

Here, I applied kmeans clustering to perform image segmentation on three pictures taken from a trip to Big Bend National Park and Marfa. I found that this method was easily adapated to each new example and could create clusters that were not limited to a predefined geometric form (circle, triangle, etc). In each image, pixels were assigned clusters based on (Exp 1) their rgb color profile and (Exp 2) their rgb data and xy coordinate.

One caveat to this approach is that it relies on randomly selected starting positions for the centroids, and because of this, the clustering results varied across implementation attempts, meaning that sometimes elements of the images were optimally segmented and sometimes they were not (e.g., on image 1, the cacti in the foreground being colored green instead of brown like the mountains). Increasing k sometimes allowed for better segregation of unique elements (e.g., the sky became apparent in image 2, the parking lot and sky became apparent in image 3), but not always. For instance, image 1 had a relatively consistent color palette and clustering result across values of k.

Here is a separate example experimenting with different values of k (k=3,5,7) for segmentation of a cactus photograph:



One limitation of this method is the random start point assignment for each cluster centroid. A possibility for overcoming this that I explored was including spatial information (x, y coordinates; Exp 2). It was necessary to scale the coordinates before they were input into the k-means clustering algorithm to avoid overprioritizing the spatial features at the expense of the color features. Images with lower color variation (see below) benefitted from inclusion of spatial information.

Parameters: Both k=7, first image without spatial coordinates in algorithm, second image included spatial coordinates



From these results, I learned that the optimal value of k and the decision about whether to also include spatial information alongside color information as input to the clustering algorithm depends on image - there is no absolute best approach. Choosing k manually for each image is difficult, and because the results change from multiple trials, one value for k may perform well on one iteration but not the next (this is where setting a seed would be very important). Increasing k and/or including spatial features can improve segmentation results, but also risks overfitting data resulting clusters that are not visually distinct.

    How is brain tissue integrity associated with stability of sleep-activity patterns?

    Virtual poster presentation for the annual Organization for Human Brain Mapping international conference (2020)

    In healthy aging, older adults often report changes in their rest-activity rhythms, for example, increased morningness preference, earlier wake times, increased sleep latency, and decreased total sleep time. These patterns can be assessed objectively using wrist-based accelerometers, or actigraphs.

    We analyzed multi-shell diffusion tensor images from 103 healthy young and older adults using tract-based spatial statistics (TBSS) to examine relationships between white matter microstructure and rest-activity rhythm stability. Rest-activity rhythm measures were computed using both cosinor and non-parametric methods derived from seven days of accelerometer data. Because prior studies have suggested that the corpus callosum is sensitive to sleep physiology and rest-activity rhythm, we also conducted a focused region of interest analysis on the corpus callosum.

    Greater rest-activity rhythm stability was associated with greater brain integrity across both young and older adults, primarily in the corpus callosum and anterior corona radiata. This effect was not moderated by age group. While rest-activity rhythm measures were associated with sleep metrics, rest-activity rhythm measures uniquely accounted for the variance in white matter integrity.

    This study strengthens existing evidence for a relationship between brain white matter structure and rest-activity rhythm stability in the absence of health risk factors. While there are differences in rest-activity rhythm stability between age groups, the relationship with brain white matter was present across both young and older adults. rest-activity rhythms may be a useful biomarker of brain health across both periods of adult development.

    Facial Recognition: Can the Machine Beat the Mask?

    Dimensionality Reduction with SVM Classification



    The goal of the project was to use a dimension reduction approach (PCA, etc.) to decompose and transform high-dimension image data into a (much) lower dimension space for classification and clustering. I used data collected from my machine learning class in 2021. Each student submitted photographs of themselves with four different emotions: anger, sadness, happiness, and fear. For each emotion, we took one photograph with a mask on, and one photograph without, resulting in a total of 8 photographs per student.

    My goal was to find the best two eigenvectors that defined a 2D space to best separate categories on three different tasks: emotion recognition, person recognition, and mask detection. I visualized individual data points in a 2D plot and calculate a Silhouette score for each subtask.

    I implemented a few different image processing techniques, including edge detection and cropping, to try to maximize the amount of useful information to facilitate cluster separation.

    How Valid Are Fitbit Sleep Metrics?

    Using Bland-Altman analysis and repeated measure correlations (2021)

    Currently, the potential of leveraging wearable technology for sleep-activity research is limited by the lack of existing validity studies. The purpose of this project was to compare the ability of activity and sleep tracking technologies to accurately classify aspects of the rest-activity rhythm. We compared sleep and rest-activity rhythm measures derived from activity and step count data collected from the Actiwatch 2.0 and one of two sleep-staging Fitbit models, the Charge 2 or the Inspire HR. The two devices were worn by participants simultaneously for ten to fourteen days. We quantified rest-activity rhythms using both parametric (Marler, 2006) and non-parametric (Blume, 2016) methods.

    Relative to the Actiwatch, Fitbit showed good agreement on rest-activity measures calculated using the extended cosinor method, only over- or under- estimating these metrics by less than 2\%. However, non-parametric measures showed less agreement across devices, with agreement estimates ranging from 9.05\% to 27.41\%. We found large differences in Fitbit estimation of mean daily TST and TIB, both of which were underestimated by the Fitbit by over 70 minutes.
