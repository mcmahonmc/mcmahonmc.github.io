URL Listing

Simplifying Cross-Listing for Mercari's Power Sellers

Overview

Mercari wanted to enable experienced sellers to easily list many items at once. We knew that many experienced sellers cross-post, using multiple resale platforms to maximize their exposure and increase their chances of making a sale. During Mercari’s bi-annual hackathon, Go Bold Day, an engineer proposed an idea to simplify cross-posting by developing an import tool that could pull metadata from other sites and easily populate Mercari’s listing form. The idea won the company-wide hackathon and was developed into a product aimed at power sellers.

This case study outlines the research conducted to improve the import listing tool's usability and optimize the experience for sellers, resulting in significant improvements in listing numbers and seller adoption.

My Role

As the lead UX researcher for this project, I was responsible for: designing and conducting remote usability tests with experienced sellers, synthesizing data into actionable insights and recommendations, collaborating with design and engineering teams to implement changes based on findings, tracking key metrics post-launch to assess the impact of the usability improvements.

Outcome

The research and design improvements led to:

  • 3x increase in new daily listings.
  • 395,000 new listings in the first month after launch.
  • 1,000 new power sellers using the import tool within the first month.
These metrics demonstrated that the changes directly contributed to increased seller confidence, improved user flow, and enhanced trust in the platform’s cross-posting capabilities.

Approach

Study design and recruitment

I recruited 8 experienced eBay sellers, each of whom listed at least 30 items per month. These users were ideal for testing the new cross-posting import tool, as they regularly manage large inventories across multiple platforms.

Contextual Inquiry

Before jumping into design and usability testing, I felt it was important to dig into power sellers' current workflows for cross-listing items, and interrogate our assumptions about where they experience pain points. This taught us that power sellers are skeptical of smart listing tools due to past experience with over-promising and under-delivering. They believe it's worth it to spend the extra time listing items initially to avoid blow-back later. From this, we learned that being able to seamlessly review items, including the descriptions, photos, and shipping details, would be critical for sellers to adopt the new feature.

Power sellers often copied listing descriptions from their own listings, or other listings on Mercari. This raised the question about whether listing templates might be more useful than a cross-listing tool.

We also learned that many power sellers who list items themselves, as opposed to through third-party platforms, do so using the Mercari app for ease of use. This contradicted our assumption that they primarily listed using web.

Usability Testing

After the contextual inquiry study, I ran remote usability tests using static images of the working design for the import listing feature. Participants were asked to “think aloud” while interacting with the new import experience. This helped us understand the sellers' thought processes and the challenges they faced during the import.

The usability test revealed several key pain points across the core tasks of the URL import tool:

  • Task 1: Complete Import Form. Users were unclear about which URL to input, expressed a desire to select different shipping options for individual items, and lacked awareness of the review step. Some participants mistakenly clicked the "Enter URL" breadcrumb, assuming it was a button.
  • Task 2: Review Import. Participants were suspicious about having to input URLs before account registration, leading to trust issues.
  • Task 3: Activate Listings. Users struggled with not being able to view shipping information for each item, were unaware of bulk edit functionality, and had difficulty changing imported metadata.
  • Task 4: Access Listings. Users clicked into listings to change metadata individually and were unaware that bulk edits could be applied to multiple listings at once.

Data Analysis

In parallel, I used EyeQuant’s Visual Attention Analysis to prompt discussion about the content clarity and user attention across key screens.

Recommendations

Based on the findings, I made several key design recommendations:

  • Separate user flows for each import platform to reduce confusion.
  • Enhance visual hierarchy and clarity through improved breadcrumbs and tooltips.
  • Move shipping options to later in the flow, after listings have been imported.
  • Provide educational content (e.g., FAQs) to help guide sellers through the process.

Reflection

This project underscored the importance of user-centered design in improving complex workflows. By observing users in real-world contexts and addressing their pain points through iterative improvements, we were able to not only meet but exceed our business goals. The most valuable lesson was the need for clarity in both language and visual design, especially when introducing new tools to experienced users who have entrenched workflows.

Additionally, we found that trust-building elements, like providing clear explanations for URL inputs and visual feedback on import progress, were crucial in improving the overall experience and driving higher adoption rates.
Before and after: Import progress

Unfortunately, development time was not allocated to improve the process for reviewing listings post-import in advance as recommended. So, when this feature launched, we saw a tremendous increase in new listings, but many were sitting in sellers' drafts folders, not yet available on the marketplace. Post-research handoff, I was moved to a new project team, which made it difficult to advocate for action on this key research finding. This to me demonstrated the opportunity for me as a researcher to guide more collaborative ideation and discussion post-research handoff, although balancing competing project priorities remains a challenge when there are so many interesting things to work on!

Post-launch, daily draft listings increased disproportionately to the total number of new listings, indicating a breakdown in listing activation.
