Designing the Future of Health

Educating Future Innovators in Healthcare Design

Teaching Innovation in Healthcare

"Designing the Futures of Health" is a multidisciplinary course at the intersection of healthcare, design, and innovation. As part of the Health Innovation Lab at VCU, this course empowers students to tackle real-world challenges in rural health systems using design thinking. By working closely with rural community hospitals, the students gain hands-on experience addressing critical issues, such as patients leaving against medical advice (AMA) and improving emergency department workflows. Through this course, we aim to create a lasting impact on underserved healthcare systems while equipping students with practical skills in human-centered design and innovation.

Learn more about the VCU da Vinci Center for Innovation.

My Role

As the course co-instructor, I lead students through the design thinking process, helping them apply these methods to healthcare challenges. I guide them through empathy research, systems mapping, ideation, and prototyping as they work on real-world projects with rural community hospitals. My role is to facilitate their learning, provide feedback, and ensure that their designs are rooted in a deep understanding of the complexities of healthcare delivery in resource-limited settings.

The Challenge

To promote experiential learning, students partnered with two rural community health systems to reduce the number of emergency department patients leaving without being seen.

Emergency department waiting room at VCU Tappahannock.

Teaching Design Thinking

At the start of the course, students are introduced to design thinking, focusing on empathy as a foundation for innovation. Students conduct interviews and observations in rural emergency departments, gathering insights directly from patients and healthcare staff. This allows them to immerse themselves in the environment and gain a deep understanding of the human factors contributing to the challenges in patient care.

Students gather and synthesize insights from interviews and direct observation.

Systems Mapping & Problem Definition

Students use systems thinking to map out the emergency department’s processes, identifying pain points and bottlenecks in patient flow and staff workflows. This exercise helps students frame the problem holistically and see where intervention is most needed. Systems maps become a key tool for collaborating with hospital staff to ensure that the insights reflect the real-world complexities of rural health.

Students share each step of their process with the health systems, seeking input and collaboration as they progress.

Co-Creation with Healthcare Providers and Staff

In the next phase, students co-create with healthcare providers, encouraging a collaborative approach to generating potential solutions. Through ideation workshops we focus on brainstorming practical, low-cost interventions that address the systemic issues uncovered during the research phase. Ideas range from improving communication between staff and patients to developing new digital tools for tracking patient flow.


Insights are shared across both hospital sites, each of which has unique needs.


We use innovative methods, like Design Thinking and LEGO Serious Play, to facilitate creativity and collaboration.

Prototyping and Iterative Testing

With ideas in hand, students develop prototypes that are tested and refined in collaboration with hospital staff. This hands-on process allows students to iterate based on feedback and real-world use. Solutions are designed to be implementable within the constraints of rural healthcare, ensuring that they are scalable and sustainable over time.

VCU da Vinci Center Maker Space.

Student Impact

  • Systems thinking. Students utilize a systems thinking approach to gain a comprehensive understanding of the intricate challenges within our local health systems, enabling more effective and sustainable solutions.
  • Patient retention solutions. Students take the lead on developing strategies to reducing the number of patients leaving without being seen in rural community hospital emergency departments.
  • Health system collaboration. The partnership between students and rural healthcare providers fosters a culture of innovation in these hospitals, demonstrating that even small changes can have a big impact.
