Wrist Rhythms

Testing Fitbit Potential for Capturing Circadian Rhythms

Overview

This study explored the use of Fitbit as a tool to assess circadian rest-activity rhythms in young and older adults, comparing it to the gold-standard Actiwatch. While commercial devices like Fitbit are popular in sleep assessment, their validation across diverse age groups is limited. This project addressed that gap by rigorously evaluating Fitbits in both young and older populations, with implications for the future of digital health.

The Challenge

With an increasing number of consumers using wearables to monitor sleep and activity patterns, it is crucial to validate the data accuracy for these commercial devices. The traditional gold standard, polysomnography (PSG), is limited in its ability to track long-term rest-activity rhythms. Although actigraphy is a well-validated alternative, its use in everyday settings is cumbersome and costly. This study aimed to validate Fitbit’s capacity to measure rest-activity rhythms across young and older populations in an ecologically valid setting.

Fitbits Perform Well in Capturing Circadian Rhythms but Not Sleep Time / UT News

Approach

To ensure a robust comparison, we recruited two groups—29 young adults and 29 older adults—with strict inclusion criteria to eliminate confounding sleep disturbances. Randomization was employed in assigning which wrist wore the Actiwatch or Fitbit to mitigate handedness effects. I designed the recruitment process to ensure that participants represented a healthy demographic, free from significant sleep disorders, and excluded those on psychiatric medications to prevent interference with sleep-wake patterns.

Key Decisions

  • Participant Recruitment. Recruitment targeted adults aged 18-30 for young adults and 60-89 for older adults. Participants were screened for sleep quality using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) and a psychomotor vigilance task to assess alertness, ensuring that only those without significant sleep disruptions were included.
  • Randomization and Device Assignment. Randomizing wrist assignment for each device minimized the effects of handedness on activity monitoring. Both devices were worn for 10-14 days to gather comprehensive data on participants' typical rest-activity rhythms. By using a longer wear time, I ensured a high degree of ecological validity while accounting for day-of-week variability.
  • Data Collection and Preprocessing. Data preprocessing involved handling missing values through linear interpolation and carefully reviewing participants’ sleep diaries to account for periods when devices were removed. Analyzing 60-second epochs of activity data from both devices, I normalized each subject’s activity levels to enable accurate cross-device comparisons, ensuring robust statistical analysis.

Analysis and Findings

Fitbits exhibited strong agreement with Actiwatch on global rest-activity rhythm measures like amplitude and acrophase but underestimated sleep metrics such as total sleep time (TST) and time in bed (TIB) by over 70 minutes. Bland-Altman plots and repeated measure correlations revealed that the discrepancies were more pronounced in older adults, which suggests the need for further research into wearable technology’s application in this population.

Despite these discrepancies, the study concluded that Fitbit provides strong instrumental validity for capturing rest-activity rhythms, especially over multi-day periods. This finding opens doors to the broader application of Fitbit devices in long-term digital health monitoring, particularly in older populations, where changes in rest-activity rhythms can signal cognitive or physical decline.

Reflection and Learnings

One major challenge was balancing the ecological validity of the study with the need for rigorous, high-quality data. The decision to use sleep diaries helped improve data accuracy, but missing data from device removal still posed issues. Additionally, the observed discrepancies in sleep metrics underscored the importance of cross-validation with polysomnography (PSG) in future studies, particularly for older adults.

Impact on Digital Health

This study confirmed that while Fitbit may not be as precise as Actiwatch in measuring nightly sleep metrics, it remains a promising tool for monitoring long-term rest-activity rhythms. Given Fitbit’s scalability, low cost, and wide adoption, its application in digital health, especially for monitoring sleep-related cognitive and physical health issues in older adults, is promising. The findings contribute to ongoing discussions about leveraging commercial wearables for large-scale health monitoring and clinical research.

This research is now published in Smart Health, 29 (2023).
